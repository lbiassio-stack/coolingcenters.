Heat Vulnerability and Cooling Center Hotspot

These two maps examine how cooling center availability in Seattle intersects with census-level racial and social disadvantage. The first map overlays the locations of cooling facilities against Seattle's Racial and Social Equity Composite Index, a census-tract measure of cumulative disadvantage based on race, income, language, and health outcomes. While cooling centers are distributed across the city, a clear north-south divide emerges. North Seattle neighborhoods with lower disadvantage scores tend to have comparatively better facility coverage, while high-disadvantage communities in South Seattle, including South Park, Georgetown, and parts of the Rainier Valley, show sparser infrastructure despite having the greatest vulnerability to extreme heat. The second map, a hotspot analysis, confirms this unevenness at the census-tract level. The vast majority of Seattle's tracts register the lowest concentration of cooling facilities, with meaningful hotspots appearing only in isolated pockets near Greenwood, Rainier Beach, and central Seattle. Together, these maps reveal that cooling center access in Seattle is neither evenly distributed nor consistently aligned with where it is needed most, which can be used to inform planning policies to advance equitable climate preparedness as the city faces a future of more frequent and more intense heat events.
