Cooling Center accessibility in Seattle

Extreme heat events are becoming more frequent and intense as climate change progresses, creating increasing public health risks in cities. Cooling centers (publicly accessible, air-conditioned buildings) play a critical role in protecting residents during extreme heat. Potential cooling centers include libraries, community centers, public schools, and other public buildings where residents may be able to seek relief during heat waves.

This website is a tool for Seattle residents to identify facilities they can access during extreme temperature events. By mapping these locations across the city, we aim to better understand the spatial distribution of cooling infrastructure.

How to use this website

As you see on the tabs above, there are two other pages you can access here.

"Cooling center access" compares the distribution of cooling centers with demographic and socioeconomic characteristics across Seattle.

"Find the nearest cooling center" gives users the opportunity to enter an address to estimate the distance to the nearest cooling center. In this pilot project, accessibility is operationalized using Euclidean distance between locations, recognizing that transportation systems may be disrupted during extreme weather conditions.

Sources

The spatial database used for this project integrates multiple GIS datasets. Facility data includes Seattle Parks and Recreation community centers, pools, spray parks, wading pools, lifeguard beaches, and public libraries. Datasets are sourced from the City of Seattle’s open GIS portals. Transit accessibility is represented using route data from regional transit datasets provided by King County, allowing analysis of proximity to frequent transit near potential cooling centers.

Demographic characteristics are incorporated using American Community Survey data at the census tract level, including population, race, and income indicators.
